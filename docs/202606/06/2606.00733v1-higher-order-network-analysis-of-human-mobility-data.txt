Abstract 

The detailed study of individual human mobility requires large-scale high-resolution datasets, but collecting such datasets in a way that is both statistically powerful and privacy preserving is a challenging and expensive task. In response, researchers have built tools to generate complex synthetic populations of agents that can be used to simulate synthetic individual mobility data, potentially obvi-ating the difficulties of data collection. While these simulation-based approaches offer a promising avenue for expanding individual mobility research, it is difficult to asses whether such tools are effective at generating realistic mobility traces. In this work, we develop a framework for comparing observed and simulated mobility data using a higher-order network framework that focuses on analyz-ing patterns of movement in the paths individuals take through the underlying infrastructure network. We apply our framework to a case study comparing the NetMob 2025 Data Challenge Dataset, which includes individual mobility data for thousands of residents of the ˆIle-de-France region, with a sophisticated open-source synthetic population and mobility simulation model of the same region. We show that while simulated mobility data is indeed promising as a surrogate for observed mobility, there are some key limitations to the simulation paradigm from a path-based perspective, which we discuss along with potential future remediations and open challenges for higher-order mobility network analysis.

# 1 Introduction 

Understanding mobility dynamics in transportation networks is critical for many engineering and policymaking tasks, as well as deeper questions about how humans move through our environment [1, 2]. However, the analysis of human mobility data presents unique challenges due to the diversity of human movement across scales, the difficulty of collecting representative data that is high-resolution in both space and time, and the methodological and computational challenges in analyzing such high-dimensional spatio-temporal datasets, all while protecting the privacy of individual participants [1, 3–5]. These challenges have led to research on how to model human behavior without collecting large-scale data through the use of synthetic populations and mobility simulations [6–10]. Ideally, simulated data based on population-level mobility surveys can be leveraged to develop detailed models of mobility without col-lecting data about the detailed movements of individuals [10]. However, the validity and correspondence of simulated datasets to real-world mobility must be systemati-cally evaluated to ensure that conclusions drawn from simulations can be generalized to understand real human mobility patterns. In this work, we develop a framework for analyzing human mobility data using 

higher-order network science and explore how this framework can be used to evalu-ate the validity of simulated mobility datasets [11, 12]. The key difference between traditional network-based models and the higher-order approach we take here is the treatment of memory . Traditional network models often implicitly assume that move-ment between nodes is memoryless, meaning that the next step is based only on the current position. This assumption limits the ability of these methods to capture sequential dependencies inherent to real-world mobility data, for example those that arise from the intentionality of human movement from origins ( e.g. , home) toward des-tinations ( e.g. , work or school). Empirical analyses of transportation systems indicate the existence of memory effects in routing decisions, whereby choices are influenced not only by current state, but also on the sequence of previously traversed nodes [13– 15]. Therefore it is important to understand the extent to which simulated mobility datasets accurately reproduce these higher-order dependencies. The higher-order net-work representations we use in this work incorporate memory topologically, allowing for the natural generalization of traditional methods for analysis of directed and weighted networks to higher-order patterns [11, 12]. In this work we take as a case study the ˆIle-de-France region of France. This region is ideal for our analysis due to the availability of two key data sources. First, a recent survey and associated dataset made available through the 2025 NetMob Data Challenge provide a high-quality representative sample of real GPS trajectories representing individual human mobility in the region [16]. Second, a well-developed synthetic population model and computational simulation environment designed to simulate human mobility in the ˆIle-de-France region provides a testbed for generating synthetic mobility traces [10]. The availability of these two data sources makes it pos-sible to make a detailed comparison of higher-order movement patterns in both real and simulated mobility datasets in the same region. We make four primary contributions in this paper: 21. We build a map-matching pipeline to convert raw GPS traces from the 2025 Net-Mob Data Challenge Dataset [16] representing movement through the ˆIle-de-France region into sequences of nodes visited in a network ( i.e. , network walks or paths ). 2. We use the Open ˆIle-de-France MATSim simulation software [10] to generate a synthetic population and simulated mobility dataset, extracting paths of agent mobility through the network from the simulation output. 3. Taking these extracted mobility paths as our unit of analysis, we apply techniques from higher-order network analysis to compare the observed and simulated datasets from a path-based perspective, identifying key divergences between the real-world and simulated paths. 4. We outline some implications of these divergences for human mobility research using surrogate data from agent-based simulations.  

> Fig. 1 Map showing the coverage of the transportation network across the ˆIle-de-France region for both NetMob and MATSim data. The inset plot zooms in on the region from Bois de Boulogne in the west to the area surrounding the Louvre in the east.

Our results show that, while the MATSim simulated mobility paths broadly repro-duce many patterns in the observed NetMob Data Challenge dataset, they also diverge in a few key ways because the simulations: (1) do not reproduce paths lengths at the extreme end of the observed data; (2) produce different node visitation patterns; and (3) are on the whole more predictable than the observed mobility paths. We also identify some key challenges moving forward, including the need for improved pre-processing pipelines for GPS trajectory data, as well as more detailed simulation 3models for pedestrian and cyclist behavior that are flexible enough to incorpo-rate diverse mobility behavior beyond commutes, especially one-off trips and leisure activities. Our approach is complementary to related research primarily due to our focus on the analysis of how people (and agents) navigate the detailed topology of the underly-ing physical infrastructure. This is in contrast to human mobility research that encodes mobility patterns in the geospatial domain, without connecting spatial movements to an underlying physical network ( e.g. , [17–25]). Our work is also different from research that focuses on the analysis of visits to points-of-interest (POI), such as shops, restau-rants, and hospitals ( e.g. , [26–32]) for the same reason. Other related research has analyzed the topological structure of street networks [21, 33–35], and while our work takes these street networks as the underlying topology facilitating mobility, we further analyze the interplay between network structure and higher-order mobility dynamics. Finally, closely related recent work also analyzes the 2025 NetMob Data Challenge dataset, combining many of the above areas with a focus on linking features of street networks and the built environment with pedestrian mobility patterns at a 1km ×1km geospatially aggregated scale [36]. Again our work takes a different approach, focusing on the detailed usage of the underlying network, rather than aggregating to a larger geospatial unit or collection of POIs. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe the two datasets that we analyze in this work, including the pre-processing pipeline required to transform the raw datasets into paths through the underlying network. In Section 3, we define our methodology for analyzing human mobility data using higher-order network science. Then, in Section 4 we describe the results of our analyses. In Section 5, we discuss our findings and their implications before finally concluding the paper with prospects for future research in Section 6. 

# 2 Datasets 

In this work we analyze two datasets representing human mobility in the ˆIle-de-France region. In both datasets we focus our analysis on road transportation modes, including travel by car, bus, commercial vehicle, bicycle, and walking. We exclude transportation via train and metro from our analysis, primarily due to the fact that these modes are inherently difficult to track using GPS, since they may travel underground leading to large noise in the data due to temporary loss of signal. In the next two sections, we describe each dataset, as well as our processing pipeline to extract paths through the network. 

## 2.1 NetMob 2025 Dataset 

The 2025 NetMob Data Challenge Dataset contains mobility trajectories for 3,320 people living in the ˆIle-de-France region [16]. Each participant volunteered to carry a dedicated GPS device and log the purpose of each trip they took during a study period of 7 days. The dataset includes surveyed demographic data about individual participants as well as metadata for trips and geographic regions. 4The dataset represents the movements of participants around the ˆIle-de-France region as sequences of GPS coordinates representing participant position over time. These sequences are logically split into trips based on logs kept by the participants, meaning that for each participant we have a series of GPS traces corresponding to trips taken over the study period. The data have been pre-processed for outliers, missing data, and anonymity, including the application of spatial blurring to the GPS traces to avoid revealing primary home and work locations of participants (see section 3 of [16] for details). In total, the dataset includes over 80,000 trips taken by 3,320 participants over periods of 7 days between October 2022 and May 2023. Each trip also includes the date, start and end time, primary mode of travel ( e.g. private car, taxi, or bus), and the purpose of the trip. Since our goal is to analyze mobility represented as walks through a network, we need to transform these raw GPS traces into sequences of edges traversed through the mobility network of the region, a process which we describe in the next section. 

2.1.1 Map matching 

The transformation of the mobility traces from sequences of GPS coordinates to sequences of network nodes is known as map matching [37, 38]. Map matching transforms raw GPS traces, which are inherently noisy representations of device mea-surements, by matching each coordinate to the most likely road segment or intersection in an underlying geospatial network. The map matching procedure requires an underlying network to which the coor-dinates will be assigned. We use the OpenStreetMaps (OSM) [39] street network of the ˆIle-de-France region as of January 1st, 2022 available via GeoFabrik. 1 An OSM network describes in detail the position and geometry of elements like road segments, intersections, sidewalk availability, bicycle paths, rail lines, and so on. We will be pri-marily interested in the road network, consisting of all OSM nodes that are included in “ways” with the tag “highway”, which entails most public surface roads. We match the NetMob GPS trajectories to the OSM network using the Hidden Markov Model technique, which is, to the best of our knowledge, the gold standard methodology for map matching [37, 38]. We use the implementation provided by Valhalla, an open source routing engine for OSM data [40]. Map matching is a difficult task due to the inherent unreliability of GPS mea-surements. Many factors can make matching difficult, including outlier coordinates, missing data, or complex network geometries leading to ambiguity that is not eas-ily resolvable in an automatic way. The Valhalla matcher was unable to find matches for a number of input trajectories. While we did some experimentation to try to get the best matching possible, ultimately we decided to discard the 3,692 trajectories for which we could not get a match. We leave evaluating and improving the map matching step further for future work. Once we have assigned each GPS coordinate in a trip to an edge in the road network, we take the deduplicated sequence of edges to be the path of the participant through the network. ## 2.2 ˆ Ile-de-France MATSim simulation 

We compare the NetMob 2025 Data Challenge Dataset to a second dataset gener-ated by the simulation software MATSim, a state-of-the-art iterative, agent-based methodology for dynamic traffic assignment [6–10]. We take advantage of the open-source ˆIle-de-France MATSim synthetic popula-tion generation and MATSim simulation code made available by Horl et al. [10]. 2 This software provides code to generate a detailed synthetic population of ˆIle-de-France that is compatible with the MATSim simulation software, as well as well as an inte-grated pipeline for generating the population and running the software. Constructing the synthetic population of ˆIle-de-France and running the MATSim simulation takes advantage of a variety of public regulatory data as documented in the paper describ-ing the simulation software [10]. The output of the MATSim simulation includes data about how the synthetic agents moved throughout the network, which we process into paths, as well as which travel mode the agents used. The MATSim software generates simulated movements of agents through the net-work in the form of an event log. We process this event log to extract the detailed

While the MATSim software begins from the same underlying OpenStreetMaps network, a simplified form of the network where some nodes and edges are removed is used in the simulation. Since the nodes retained in the MATSim network are still OpenStreetMaps identifiers, we can implement this interpolation by scanning each edge of each trip and checking if it is an edge in our detailed network. In MATSim, walking and bicycle modes are implemented as teleportation from an origin to destination node. Therefore, we need to choose how to model walking and biking movements. We again use a Dijkstra shortest path algorithm with the length of the underlying geometry as the edge weight to find these paths. In future studies it would be interesting to experiment with more detailed simulation models for pedestrian and cyclist mobility. Finally, for public transportation trajectories we filter out all modes except for buses, since we are modeling only road-based transportation. The underlying MAT-Sim simulation uses the real bus schedule for the region in Generalized Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) format to route agents through the transportation network. The GTFS schedule involves OSM network nodes representing transit stops, which are not part of our underlying network. However, we can simply map each bus stop to its associated link in the network to get the path in terms of the OSM road network nodes. ## 2.3 Random Walk Dataset 

We compare the NetMob and MATSim simulated datasets against a third dataset constructed by running a memoryless random walk on the same underlying network. We design this random walk dataset to mimic the combined path statistics of the two datasets by generating one random walk for each path in the two datasets (right-hand column in Table 1). For each path, we select its starting node as the starting node in the random walk, then let the walker take exactly the same number of steps as the original path. In this way, the final path dataset has the same path length distribution and starting node distribution as the combination of the two datasets. 72.4 Network Simplification 

It is common to simplify the topology of OpenStreetMaps networks for network analyses [35]. While we use the detailed OSM road network for the purposes of map-matching, path extraction, and random walk generation, as a final pre-processing step we drop from our paths “redundant” nodes that do not represent true junctions in the network. The most common redundant nodes appear in the middle of chains repre-senting road segments without any turns, meaning they offer no meaningful choice in a transportation context (except perhaps in the rare case of a U-turn). These nodes are usually included in the network for other reasons, for example to represent pedestrian crossings, complex lane topologies, or simply to strike a balance between representing network routing and preserving the geometry of the underlying geospatial network [35]. Redundant nodes can be removed without altering the ability of a person or agent to traverse the network. In the directed context, we identify redundant nodes as those with exactly the same 2 neighbors in their in- and out-neighborhood (or different sin-gle neighbors in the case of a one-way road). Removing such a node and replacing it with edges between its former neighbors does not alter the network connectivity with respect to navigation, since paths between all other origins and destinations are pre-served. Since the removal of one redundant node will change the neighborhood of its former neighbors, the filtering process is non-independent and the order in which nodes are processed can determine which nodes are labeled redundant. Therefore, it is impor-tant that all redundant nodes are identified in the network as a pre-processing step before those nodes are subsequently dropped from the path datasets. If the removal of redundant nodes results in an empty path, we drop that path from the data. The original network had 3,016,582 nodes and 6,655,074 edges. After filtering, the network was reduced to 788,018 nodes (26% of original) and 2,197,946 edges (33% of original). In the NetMob paths, we identified 788,685 unique redundant nodes and removed a total of 7,601,189 occurrences, which resulted in the exclusion of 657 paths. In the MATSim paths, we identified 588,606 unique redundant nodes, removed a total of 6,115,998 occurrences, and excluded 370 MATSim paths. Finally, in the random walk dataset, we identified 986,182 redundant nodes, removed a total of 12,551,316 occurrences, and excluded 2,341 Random Walk paths. Note that the path counts in Table 1 (and all subsequent results we will discuss, unless otherwise specified) reflect the paths after applying this simplification step. 

# 3 Methods 

In this section we describe our approach to analyzing the resulting paths using net-work science methods. First, we provide background on the three repesentations that underly all of our methods: networks, de Bruijn graphs, and multi-order network mod-els. Then, we describe the metrics we use in conjunction with these representations to understand how people and agents move through transportation systems. 83.1 Network representations 

We begin by defining the network representations we will use throughout the rest of this work, beginning with a traditional network:

In the context of human mobility, nodes will typically correspond to intersections or junctions in a transportation network, while links correspond to conduits for moving between them, primarily roads. Since some roads can only be traversed in a specific direction, we will consider the network to be directed. The total number of links connected to a node is called its degree . The total degree is the sum of the number of incoming links ( in-degree ) and the number of outgoing links (out-degree ). The nodes that have edges pointing towards u are called its in-neighbors 

and the nodes that u has edges to are called its out-neighbors .

3.1.1 Networks, walks, and paths 

In this study we are interested in understanding how people and agents move through a mobility network. Traversals of networks can be formally defined as walks , which are sequences of nodes visited by following the links of the network. A walk that never visits the same node twice is called a path . Given two nodes u and v, a shortest directed path from u to v is a path that visits the smallest possible number of nodes. In this paper, we will only distinguish between walks and paths in the context of shortest paths, which we will always refer to explicitly, and otherwise we will use the word “path” colloquially to refer to any traversal of nodes through a network. While important to the analysis of networks, shortest paths are not the only way that one may navigate a network, but rather represent the most efficient navigation with respect to the number of links traversed and nodes visited. The simplest dynami-cal process for modelling the movement of an agent through a network without regard for efficiency is the discrete time random walk. A random walk is a stochastic process where at each time step t a walker is in a certain state s(t) = u ∈ V that corresponds to the walker sitting on a given node u in the network. The walker takes a step at each time point by randomly moving to an out-neighbor of the current node u.Random walks are a powerful framework for understanding how networks can function as conduits for spread and diffusion [41–43]. However, in the context of human mobility they are limited by the fact that they are memoryless , since the next step chosen by the walker depends only on its current state without regard for the nodes it visited before [11, 44]. Often this does not reflect how people navigate in their daily lives, since we do not expect a driver to randomly move between nodes in a road network, but rather we expect they will begin at an origin and move towards a destination in an efficient way. For this reason, our analysis will leverage higher-order 9network representations that explicitly account for sequential ordering and memory in the navigation of a network.

## 3.2 Analysis methods 

Now that we have defined our representations, we will use this section to describe the methods we leverage in our data analysis. 

3.2.1 Network analysis 

We analyze network statistics including the number of nodes, number of edges, average degree, and network density in empirical de Bruijn graphs of increasing order. Beyond the 1st-order network, all of these statistics provide information about the structure of the paths taken through the network, rather than only the network topology. For example, in the 2nd-order network, the number of nodes is the same as the number of edges in the 1st-order network, while the number of edges counts the number of observed paths of length 3 edges. Similarly, the out-degree of a 2nd-order node u

corresponds to the number of different paths of length 3 observed in the data. Put another way, the out-degree of u counts the number of 2-edge paths with u as the prefix. The density of a network is the total number of edges divided by the total number of possible edges, which in a directed graph is just n(n − 1), where n is the number of nodes. In a kth-order empirical de Bruijn graph, we define the density to be the total number of edges (length k paths) divided by the total possible edges given the topology of the empirical k − 1st-order de Bruijn graph. We use the k − 1st-order network because the total possible paths given the 1st-order network, regardless of whether those paths were observed in the data, can grow exponentially, leading to extremely small values of density that are more difficult to interpret. 

3.2.2 Network coverage 

We are interested in the extent to which the paths in our datasets cover the nodes in the underlying network. We count the number of times each node is visited by a participant or agent in the datasets, thus analyzing the extent to which the observed mobility traces cover the underlying physical infrastructure network. Practically, we construct a vector with an entry for each node in our study area and record the number of visits it received from participants in the NetMob data and from agents in the MATSim data. We report three quantities in this analysis. First, we compute the fraction of nodes in our the network that were visited by at least one agent. This quantifies the extent to which the network is explored. Second, we compute the Jaccard Index between the sets of nodes visited in each dataset, quantifying the extent to which the nodes visited by the datasets are the same. Third, we record the total number of visits to each node in each respective dataset to understand whether there is a correlation between highly visited nodes. In an individual dataset, the node visitation patterns of participants or agents is an indication of how much of the network is actually used. Concentration or lack of node 12 visitations could reflect intentional constraints, such as diversions of vehicle traffic away from pedestrianized areas or low emissions zones and towards specific routes. Lack of coverage may also reflect barriers to accessibility in some parts of the network, or an undersampling of participants or agents who are likely to visit those nodes. If coverage results between the observed participant data and agent based simulation match well, this is a positive signal that the simulation may serve as a good surrogate for real data. However, if there is very little agreement between the coverage results, this may indicate a fundamental discrepancy between the simulation and the observed data. 

3.2.3 Optimal order estimation 

Higher-order network models allow us to study memory effects in path data at any chosen order k. One key question is whether there is an optimal memory length that explains the observed data. We adopt a Likelihood Ratio Test between higher-order models with varying memory length k to determine the optimal model order [11]. The idea behind the likelihood ratio test is to strike a balance between model fit and model complexity. In general, we expect that increasing the order of the model will increase the likelihood, since increasing the order directly incorporates more of the data into the model. However, this increase in fit comes at the expense of additional parameters, increasing the risk of overfitting [48]. Hence the use of a Likelihood Ratio Test to decide whether a k + 1st-order model significantly improves the fit compared to a model of order k [11]. We interpret an optimal order of k = 1 as evidence that a traditional weighted and directed network is the best model for the data. In the context of human mobility, where people usually have well-defined origins and destinations, we expect to find an optimal order larger than 1. Further, in comparing mobility path datasets, it is desirable for simulated paths to have the same optimal order as representative observed path data. Even if the optimal order does not match exactly, if we find that the observed data is best explained by a model with memory, but the simulated data is best explained by a memoryless model, this would suggest that the simulated data is not a good representation of how people navigate the network. 

3.2.4 Next-step prediction 

An advantage of higher-order network models is that they naturally facilitate next-step prediction in a way that incorporates sequential memory effects embedded in path data. These predictions rely on empirical transition probabilities computed from 13 the input path data, offering a data-driven approach to predict future movements in mobility trajectories. Here we briefly describe the approach to next-step prediction we use in this paper, which is based on the methods introduced by Gote et al. [47]. Given a partial path ( v1, . . . , v ℓ) of length ℓ, we perform next-step prediction by computing the (higher-order) transition probability for each node vℓ+1 ∈ η(vℓ) that could appear next on the path according to the network topology, where we use η(vℓ)to denote the out-neighbors of the node vℓ in the underlying 1st-order graph topology. We make our prediction by selecting a node proportional to its transition probability according to a kth-order model (eq. (2)) using a softmax function. Transition proba-bilities are derived from a set of training paths that are used to construct the kth-order model. If the partial path was not observed in the training data, we try to make a “backoff” prediction using models of decreasing order k − 1, k − 2, . . . , 1. If we reach the 1st-order model, this means that none of the subpaths of the partial path were observed in the training data. In this case, we simply use a memoryless random walk step as our prediction in the absence of any higher-order evidence. If next-step prediction performs well on a dataset, we may conclude that the higher-order network model captures important sequential features of the mobility tra-jectories. In contrast, if next-step prediction performs poorly, this would suggest that the higher-order network model is not able to capture the sequential dependencies in the data and could indicate that the dataset represents mobility trajectories that are more complex, varied, or noisy.

These results show that both observed and simulated mobility paths are influenced by more than just the network topology.

4.5.1 Next-step entropy 

To better understand the predictability of mobility paths, we compute the average conditional entropy of next-step distributions for models of increasing orders. A model with high conditional entropy indicates that there is ambiguity in next-step probability distributions, while small entropy indicates that the choice of next-step is largely deter-mined in the transition probability distribution. The right-hand column of Figure 5 shows the expected conditional entropy Hk as a function of order k. The conditional entropy Hk declines monotonically with memory order for both datasets, indicating that higher-order context makes next-step choices more predictable. For every mode and every order, the NetMob paths exhibit higher Hk than the MATSim paths, implying systematically larger route variability in observed behav-ior. Notably, even as the number of nodes grows with k (which also can been seen as an increase in the number of distinct contexts), the average uncertainty per con-text shrinks, showing that higher-order conditioning partitions behavior into more specialized yet more predictable contexts. 20 5 Discussion 

Taken together, our results reveal a complex and variable relationship between the NetMob and MATSim mobility paths. On the positive side, the simulated MATSim paths broadly align with the observed NetMob paths in terms of path lengths, basic network properties, and estimated optimal order, and they both show clear and con-sistent differences from random walks without memory. However, our analysis also found that the simulated paths are different in some key ways that warrant further investigation in follow-up studies. First, we found that the simulated trajectories did not produce extreme values in the path length distribution observed in the NetMob data. Second, we found that the node visitation patterns, including the set of nodes visited and their frequency of visitation, did not align as closely as we might hope if we would like to generalize the simulation approach to regions where detailed GPS data is not available. Finally, we found that the simulated trajectories were on the whole more predictable than the observed human mobility data, suggesting that real mobility behavior, while far from random, is more noisy than these simulations allow. These three differences point to an inherent limitation of simulated mobility trajec-tories: their inability to model noisy, extreme events that are nonetheless reflections of intentional human behavior. We hypothesize that some of the differences we observed through our analyses are due to the fact that individual mobility briefly, but regularly, strays far from the norm. These aberrations are difficult to capture in a large-scale mobility model. For example, it is difficult to endow an agent with the ability to choose to help a friend move, or to visit a distant relative who lives on the opposite side of the city once per year. These sorts of events are, somewhat paradoxically, commonplace at the individual level, but difficult to model at the collective scale using a synthetic population built from survey data. We believe that further research into modeling these off-model behaviors can greatly improve the promise of simulation approaches to generating human mobility data. We also note that all of our results on the NetMob dataset hinge on the accuracy of our map matching pipeline, but in the absence of ground truth, we are not able to give an estimate of our confidence in the map matched paths. We believe that benchmarking and evaluation of map matching techniques on modern GPS data is a key methodological challenge that should be addressed in the future. Our analysis shows the promise of applying higher-order network models in human mobility research. We found that human mobility is indeed a higher-order phe-nomenon, requiring the incorporation of notions of memory. Taking a path-based perspective allowed us to model the topological and dynamical factors of human mobility together, showing how the two are intertwined. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this work we have compared two human mobility datasets from the perspective of higher-order network science, with the goal of understanding whether the dataset generated via simulations from a synthetic population is a good model for the observed NetMob 2025 Dataset. We found that, while the datasets broadly align on collective 21 human mobility trends, the simulated data was not able to capture the variation observed in the real data. Many questions remain open for future research. It is clear from the poor next-step prediction results on Bicycle and Walking paths that we need to better understand non-vehicle based mobility. In the NetMob case, this likely means further refinement of map-matching methods, as the performance of map-matching is a key remaining source of uncertainty, especially given that pedestrian movements are not constrained to the road network topology, where the underlying OSM data is most likely to be accurate. Future research would benefit from methods combining machine learning approaches to automatically segment trips based on transportation mode with improved map-matching techniques. In the MATSim case, future research should incorporate specific models for pedestrian and cyclist mobility, including those that can be tuned for leisure behaviors, rather than falling back on shortest paths between origins and destinations as we have done here. Future research could build on this analysis by incorporating further context, including the analysis of trip purpose, point-of-interest visitations, as well as adding sociodemographic variables into the analysis, all of which we have left out of scope in this work. Both of the datasets allow for weighting of mobility behavior based on population representativeness, though it will be a challenge to decide how exactly to use those weights, since the observed trajectories occur at the individual level, while weights reflect population-level statistical representativeness. Finally, the use of ran-dom walks as a baseline comparison could be expanded in numerous directions, for example by introducing biased or weighted walks, higher-order walks, or other null models suited for the analysis of human mobility. We believe this study provides a foundation for further research into using higher-order network science to compare observed and simulated mobility pathway datasets.

We ran the next-step prediction experiment again on the NetMob bicycle paths, but where we 28 first randomly downsampled the paths so they have exactly the same number as the MATSim dataset.
